Lighthouse Facts (demo document)

A lighthouse is a tower, building, or structure designed to emit light from
a system of lamps and lenses, used as a navigational aid for maritime
pilots at sea or on inland waterways.

Early lighthouses used open fires or wood-burning beacons. The Pharos of
Alexandria, built in the 3rd century BC on the island of Pharos near
Alexandria, Egypt, is one of the earliest known lighthouses and was
counted among the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World.

Modern lighthouses typically use rotating lenses (often Fresnel lenses,
invented in 1822) to concentrate light into a powerful beam visible for
many kilometers. Each lighthouse has a distinct light pattern, called its
"characteristic," so sailors can identify which lighthouse they are
looking at purely from its flashing sequence.

Since the late 20th century, most lighthouses have been automated, and
GPS has reduced (though not eliminated) their practical navigational
importance. Many retired lighthouses are preserved as historic landmarks.
